Such constant expenditure of psychological energy on the business of preserving life would be impossible if the fear of death were not as constant. The very term “self-preservation” implies an effort against some force of disintegration; the affective aspect of this is fear, fear of death.21 In other words, the fear of death must be present behind all our normal functioning, in order for the organism to be armed toward self-preservation.
